About

Homelessness is something that could happen to anybody. It could happen to you, your friends, your family. Chances are, you know someone who has been either homeless or on the cusp of being homeless. While it is an unfortunate situation that can impact anyone, it tends to disproportionately impact those who belong to a minority. This could be sexual orientation, gender identity, race, religion, nationality, mental/physical ability, or amount of education. Becoming homeless is something that nobody plans to purposefully do. The odds are stacked against homeless people and they need as much help and support as they can get. This is where you come in. You have the ability to educate yourself on how this is an issue, what can be done on a large scale to help support and house these people, and how to prevent this from happening. There are also things that you can do right now to provide relief to those that are homeless.

Race and homelessness

This is a graphic percentage makeup of the different races that are homeless. Even though black people make up only 13% of the population in America, they make up 40% of the homelessness population. This pattern repeats for American Indian/Alaska natives, native Hawaiians, and Pacific Islanders. They disproportionately make up a greater percentage of the homeless population compared to the US as a whole. The hispanic population in the US is approximately equal to the hispanic homeless population. This is all happening while white and Asian populations are significantly not as impacted by homelessness.

Learn more at https://endhomelessness.org/resource/racial-inequalities-homelessness-numbers/.

Drug abuse and homelessness

Read me!


Individuals that are homeless are more likely to use drugs compared to those not homeless. This could be due to many reasons, but one of the main ones is that it keeps them from realizing how hungry they are, how cold they are, how distressed they are. This coping mechanism can lead them to jail time, increased medical needs, mental illnesses, and substance abuse. While standard drug abuse treatment is already difficult, it becomes more difficult for those that are homeless. This can be due to a lack of funding for treatment, having no support system, having no contact information or transportation to a facility, or being denied from treatment. It also requires more work as both the homelessness AND the drug abuse need to be addressed. This leads to a cyclical effect that is extremely difficult to break.

Sexuality, gender identity, and homelessness

Whether or not someone belongs to the LGBTQ+ community also impacts the likelihood of experiencing homelessness. According to UCLA's Williams Institute, of their surveyed population, only 1.8% of the homeless population was both cisgender and heterosexual. This makes sense, as while there is more LGBTQ+ acceptance, a lot of families do not support their child being a part of that community and do not support them. There are also landlords, real estate agents, HOAs, and more that do not want someone a part of the LGBTQ+ community to live within their premises.

Read more here: https://williamsinstitute.law.ucla.edu/publications/lgbt-homelessness-us/

Education and homelessness

According to the United States Interagency Council on Homelessness, there is a 346% increased chance of homelessness for those without a high school diploma or GED. Individuals without a degree cannot recieve a job at the majority of higher paying institutions. Most places of work require a high school degree or equivalent to be employed. The USICH also states that there is no state within the US where someone can afford a "modest two-bedroom rental home" on minimum wage, even while working full-time. Without being able to afford a home even while working full time, the only option left is to be homeless.

Gender and homelessness

Gender also impacts the likelihood of ending up homeless. As of recent surveys taken in 2018 the National Alliance to End Homelessness determined that there are 210,284 homeless men compared to 106,119 homeless women. That is almost a ratio of 1:3 for homeless women compared to homeless men. Percentage wise, 70% of the homeless population is male, 29% of the homeless population is female, and 1% is transgender.
